  • AIC = Akaike Information Criterion (a model comparison metric; lower is better)
  • IQR = Interquartile Range (the range between the 25th and 75th percentile)
  • CI = Confidence Interval (the range within which the true value likely falls)
  • NegBin = Negative Binomial (a regression model family for overdispersed count data)

1. Project Overview

1.1 Research Question

Is access to ACR-accredited cardiac imaging facilities (specifically Cardiac MRI and Cardiac CT) equitably distributed across US counties? Do counties with higher social vulnerability or area deprivation have fewer facilities?

1.2 Study Design

  • Type: Cross-sectional ecologic analysis
  • Unit of analysis: US county (n = 3,144 counties across 50 states plus Washington DC)
  • Outcome: Count of ACR-accredited cardiac imaging facilities per county
  • Primary predictor: Social Vulnerability Index (SVI) percentile
  • Supplementary predictor: Area Deprivation Index (ADI) percentile
  • Stratifier: Metropolitan vs. nonmetropolitan status

1.3 Why This Matters

Cardiovascular disease is the number one cause of death in the United States. Advanced cardiac imaging (CMR and CCT) is essential for accurate diagnosis. The Deficit Reduction Act of 2005 requires ACR accreditation for Medicare reimbursement, making accreditation a practical gatekeeper for patient access. If accredited facilities are concentrated in wealthy urban areas, patients in rural or disadvantaged communities face barriers to receiving high-quality cardiac imaging.

4. Methodology

4.1 Data Processing Pipeline

  1. Step 1: Load ACR facility data. Filter to "Cardiac" module and valid statuses. Classify as CMR (Program = MRAP) or CCT (Program = CTAP).
  2. Step 2: Geocode facilities from ZIP code to county FIPS using the crosswalk. For ZIPs spanning multiple counties, assign to the county with the largest residential share.
  3. Step 3: Aggregate facility counts to the county level (one row per county, with CMR count and CCT count).
  4. Step 4: Join with SVI, RUCC, and population data. Compute per-capita rates (facilities per 100,000 adults aged 45 and older).
  5. Step 5: Flag counties with fewer than 1,000 adults aged 45 and older as excluded from rate calculations (n = 106 counties).

4.2 Statistical Methods

4.2.1 Descriptive Statistics (Table 1)

County-level facility counts and per-capita rates are summarized by SVI quartile and by metropolitan/nonmetropolitan status. We report median (IQR) and mean rates.

4.2.2 Spearman Rank Correlation

We use Spearman (not Pearson) correlation because the data is heavily skewed. Most counties have zero facilities, so the distribution is not normal. Spearman measures the monotonic relationship between facility rate and SVI (or ADI) percentile.

4.2.3 Negative Binomial Regression (Primary Analysis)

We model the facility count in each county using a Negative Binomial regression.

Model formula:
log(expected count) = intercept + coefficient * predictor + offset(log of population aged 45 and older)

Why Negative Binomial instead of Poisson?
The variance of facility counts is much larger than the mean (this is called overdispersion). Poisson regression assumes variance equals mean. Negative Binomial adds a dispersion parameter to handle the extra variance. Model comparison confirmed Negative Binomial is preferred (difference in AIC greater than 30 for both modalities).

Predictor scaling: SVI percentile is scaled per 10-percentile increment. This means the Incidence Rate Ratio (IRR) represents the multiplicative change in facility rate for every 0.10 increase in SVI (or every 10-point increase in ADI).

Offset: The natural log of the adult population aged 45 and older is used as an offset. This effectively converts the model from predicting counts to predicting rates.

4.2.4 Sensitivity Analyses

  1. Accredited-only: Exclude the 23 "Under Review" facilities
  2. SVI quartile dummies: Use categorical SVI (Q2, Q3, Q4 versus Q1 as reference) instead of continuous
  3. Metro-stratified: Run separate models for metropolitan and nonmetropolitan counties
  4. ADI as alternative predictor: Replace SVI with the Area Deprivation Index

4.2.5 ADI Construction (Singh 2003 Methodology)

The Area Deprivation Index was constructed using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) of six socioeconomic indicators at the county level:

  1. Percent of population below 150% of the federal poverty level
  2. Percent of civilian labor force that is unemployed
  3. Percent of adults aged 25 and older without a high school diploma
  4. Percent of households that are housing cost-burdened (spending more than 30% of income on housing)
  5. Median household income (inverted, so higher deprivation = lower income)
  6. Percent of children living in poverty

The first principal component explained 58.7% of the total variance across these six indicators. Raw PCA scores were then converted to national percentile ranks (0 = least deprived, 100 = most deprived).

5. Results

5.1 Descriptive Findings

  • Total facilities: 687 CMR sites across 289 counties; 1,481 CCT sites across 532 counties
  • Zero-facility counties: 90.8% of counties have NO CMR; 83.1% have NO CCT
  • Metro concentration: Metropolitan counties (37.7% of all counties) contain 98.1% of CMR and 92.4% of CCT sites
  • Table 1 medians are 0.00: This is because more than 80% of counties have zero facilities (zero-inflated distribution). The median and interquartile range reflect that the "typical" US county has no cardiac imaging facility at all.

5.5 Interpretation

Three main findings:
  1. The primary disparity is geographic (metropolitan versus rural). Metropolitan counties contain over 90% of all cardiac imaging sites despite being only 38% of counties.
  2. SVI shows no association with facility availability for either CMR or CCT. Social vulnerability (as measured by the CDC composite) does not predict where facilities are located.
  3. ADI detects a significant CMR disparity. The Area Deprivation Index, which was specifically designed for healthcare-access research, identifies a 6.3% decrease in CMR capacity for every 10-percentile increase in area deprivation. This suggests that CMR (which is more specialized and rarer) is sensitive to socioeconomic factors that SVI does not capture well.

Why ADI works where SVI does not: SVI was designed for disaster emergency response and includes themes (minority status, housing type) that may not directly relate to healthcare facility siting. ADI focuses specifically on income, education, employment, and housing quality, which are the factors that drive healthcare infrastructure investment.

8. Clarifications for Dr. Naeem

Clarification 1: Why n = 3,038 in SVI regressions versus 3,144 total counties?

The 106 excluded counties are those with fewer than 1,000 adults aged 45 and older. These very small-population counties would produce unstable per-capita rates (for example, a single facility in a county with 500 eligible adults would give a rate of 200 per 100,000, which is misleadingly high). The exclusion threshold is conservative and standard in health services research.

Clarification 2: Why are Table 1 medians all 0.00?

This is the zero-inflation in the data. 90.8% of US counties have zero CMR facilities and 83.1% have zero CCT facilities. When more than half of the values are zero, the median must be zero. This is itself the key descriptive finding: cardiac imaging is extremely concentrated in a small number of counties. Suggested footnote for the table: "Median of 0.00 reflects that more than 80% of US counties lack any ACR-accredited cardiac imaging facility (zero-inflated distribution)."

9. Limitations

  1. ACR-only scope: Does not include IAC-accredited facilities (which serve cardiology programs). For example, Utah has major academic CMR programs that are likely IAC-accredited rather than ACR-accredited.
  2. Ecologic design: County-level analysis cannot tell us about individual patients' actual access or travel burden. A patient may cross county lines to reach a facility.
  3. Cross-sectional: This is a snapshot from May 2026. We cannot assess whether access is improving or worsening over time.
  4. ZIP geocoding: Multi-county ZIP codes are resolved by residential population share, which may introduce minor misclassification.
  5. ADI at county level: The official ADI (University of Wisconsin Neighborhood Atlas) is validated at the census block group level. Our county-level construction uses the same underlying methodology (Singh 2003) but applied at a coarser geographic scale.
